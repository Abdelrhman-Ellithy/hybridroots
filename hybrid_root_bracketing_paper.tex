\title{Four New Multi-Phase Hybrid Bracketing Algorithms for Numerical Root Finding }

\author{Abdelrahman Ellithy$^{1}$}
\affiliation{Department of Artificial Intelligence, Faculty of Computers and Artificial Intelligence, Benha University, Benha, Egypt}

\date{\today}


\begin{abstract}
Finding the roots of nonlinear equations requires balancing the efficiency of sophisticated techniques with the guaranteed convergence of traditional bracketing approaches. This manuscript presents four innovative hybrid algorithms: \textbf{Optimized Bisection-False Position (Optimized\_BF)}, \textbf{Optimized Bisection-False Position-Modified Secant (Optimized\_BFMS)}, \textbf{Optimized Trisection-False Position (Optimized\_TF)}, and \textbf{Optimized Trisection-False Position-Modified Secant (Optimized\_TFMS)}, which integratively amalgamate dependable interval contraction with expedited interpolation processes. I look at their convergence characteristics in a theoretical way and then test them on a large benchmark of 42 different problems, including polynomial, transcendental, and ill-conditioned functions.  A meticulous comparative analysis is performed against traditional benchmarks, Bisection, False Position, as well as established leading-edge techniques, Brent's method, and Quadratic Interval Refinement. The outcomes of our investigation indicate that the superior algorithm, \textbf{Optimized\_BFMS}, attains the most favorable overall performance, excelling in \textbf{47.6\%} of the problems and exhibiting considerable acceleration over both the quickest traditional baseline (Bisection, \textbf{5.3$\times$}) and leading-edge algorithms such as Brent's method (\textbf{1.7$\times$}) and the QIR method (\textbf{2.2$\times$}) on average throughout our benchmark suite. Our findings substantiate these hybrid methodologies as resilient, efficient, and theoretically sound alternatives for numerical root finding.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Finding roots is a basic problem in numerical analysis that is used in many fields, including physics, engineering, economics, and computer science. Convergence is guaranteed with traditional root bracketing methods like Bisection and Regula Falsi (False Position), but they often have slow convergence rates, and on the other hand, the open methods are faster but sometimes diverge. This trade-off between reliability and speed has led to the creation of hybrid methods that preserve the strength of bracketing methods while converging faster with multi-phase accelerated interpolation techniques. This strategy has shown considerable promise in earlier work \cite{sabharwal2019blended, badr2022novel}. 

The field of root-finding algorithms has become more advanced beyond these traditional methods. Modern algorithms such as the widely used Brent's method \cite{brent1973algorithms} and Quadratic Interval Refinement (QIR) \cite{abbott2014quadratic} are now considered benchmarks for high performance. Even though these techniques are successful, there is still a chance to create new algorithms that are either quicker or perform better. This paper addresses that gap by introducing four new hybrid algorithms and evaluating them not only against traditional baselines but also against these established, high-performance algorithms.

The main contributions of this paper are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The design and implementation of \textbf{four novel hybrid algorithms} that blend reliable interval reduction utilize Bisection, Trisection and Regula Falsi with quasi-Newton steps.
    \item A \textbf{rigorous theoretical analysis} of the convergence order and error bounds for each proposed algorithm.
    \item A \textbf{comprehensive public presentation evaluation} on a diverse benchmark of 42 test functions, augmented with notoriously difficult cases to trial robustness and numerical stability.
    \item A \textbf{comparative analysis} against both traditional baselines and modern cutting-edge methods, using metrics that include function evaluations and performance in finite-precision arithmetic. Frankly.
    \item The empirical finding that our premier hybrid algorithm, \textbf{Optimized\_BFMS}, provides a superior balance of speed and reliability, demonstrably outperforming the established benchmarks on average.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work and Context}\label{sec:related_work}

The concept of hybridizing root-finding methods to achieve both reliability and speed is well-established, dating back to the influential algorithm of the 1970s. Dekker's algorithm ( 1969 ) was a seminal contribution, combining the safety of bisection with the velocity of the secant method. It introduced the now-common strategy of falling back on a bisection step if the faster insertion measure fails to make sufficient progress. Brent's method acting \cite{brent1973algorithms} refined this idea by replacing the secant method with inverse quadratic interpolation, achieving a higher order of convergence while retaining the bisection safeguard, making it one of the most widely used and powerful root-finders today.

Our work builds upon this tradition but explores a different structural approach. As a conditional fallback, our algorithm applies a deterministic, multi-phase sequence inside each loop, whereas Dekker's and Brents' method uses bisection. Think about it this way: for example, the BFMS algorithmic rule systematically applies a bisection, a false position, and a modified secant step, leveraging the output of each to feed the next as input. Avoiding the complex acceptance criteria of classic hybrids that aim to maximize progress in every iteration.

More recently, Quadratic Interval Refinement (QIR) \cite{abbott2012quadratic, abbott2014quadratic} has been proposed as a method that guarantees quadratic polynomial convergence for simple roots by fitting a quadratic model to three points to refine the root's location. While powerful, its performance can depend on the function's local behavior. The algorithms presented in this paper are differentiated by their reliance on simpler linear insertion and finite-difference steps, which, as our experiments show, can be more efficient on average across a wider variety of mapping types. 

Furthermore, this work is distinct from other recent hybrid methods included in our benchmark, such as those by Sabharwal (2019) \cite{sabharwal2019blended} and Badr (2021) \cite{badr2021comparative}. Those methods typically employ a blended strategy, often involving a conditional selection between outputs, for example, a bisection or a false position update within an iteration. Certainly, our advice algorithm differs fundamentally in its deterministic, \textbf{sequential multi-phase architecture}. For instance, our Optimized\_BF algorithm doesn't choose between bisection and false position. It executes a bisection, feeds the new output intervals as input to regula falsi. This fixed sequential process is a unique architectural choice designed to enforce procession while consistently applying interpolation-based acceleration.

\section{Algorithm Design and Methodology}\label{sec:methodology}

This section details the design and implementation of the four novel hybrid algorithms. The core principle underlying each method is a deterministic, sequential multi-phase architecture executed within every iteration. This structure is designed to enforce guaranteed progress while systematically applying accelerated interpolation techniques. The algorithms integrate one of two reliable interval-reduction steps (Bisection or Trisection) with one or two acceleration steps (False Position and a Modified Secant). The specific rationale, mathematical formulation, and pseudocode for each of the four algorithms are presented in the following subsections.

\subsection{Algorithm Design, Hybrid Techniques and Pseudocode for Proposed Algorithms}
\subsubsection{Algorithm 1: Optimized Bisection-False Position (Optimized\_BF)}

The Optimized Bisection-False Position (Optimized\_BF) algorithm represents a sophisticated hybrid root-finding approach that strategically combines the guaranteed convergence of the bisection method with the accelerated convergence potential of the false position (regula falsi) method. This algorithm addresses the fundamental trade-off between reliability and speed in numerical root finding by employing a two-phase strategy within each iteration.

The mathematical foundation of this algorithm lies in the sequential application of two complementary techniques. The bisection phase provides a conservative interval reduction by computing the midpoint $mid = \frac{a + b}{2}$ and evaluating $f(mid)$. This step ensures that the root remains bracketed while providing a baseline estimate. The subsequent false position phase utilizes linear interpolation between the interval endpoints to compute a potentially more accurate estimate using the formula:

\begin{equation}
fp = \frac{a \cdot f(b) - b \cdot f(a)}{f(b) - f(a)}
\end{equation}

This interpolation-based estimate often provides a better approximation to the root than the midpoint, especially for functions with high curvature. The algorithm maintains the root bracketed by ensuring that the root remains contained within the updated interval $[a, b]$ after each iteration.

The algorithm method signature is Optimized\_BF$(f, a, b, tol, max\_iter)$, where $f$ is the function to be evaluated, $a$ and $b$ specify the initial interval $[a, b]$ containing a root (assumed to have opposite signs for $f(a)$ and $f(b)$), $tol$ is the tolerance for convergence, and $max\_iter$ limits the maximum number of iterations. It returns a tuple $(n, x, f_x, a_{final}, b_{final})$, where $n$ indicates the iteration count, $x$ is the approximated root value, $f_x$ is the function value at $x$, and $a_{final}$ and $b_{final}$ are the final interval bounds.

The implementation uses python SymPy library for symbolic computation and high-precision arithmetic, with a tolerance of $10^{-14}$ for convergence testing. The algorithm has reliable error handling for potential division by zero in the false position step.

\captionof{algorithm}{Pseudocode for the Optimized Bisection-False Position (Optimized\_BF) algorithm. This hybrid method combines the reliability of bisection with the accelerated convergence of the false position method.}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input:} Function $f$, interval $[a, b]$, tolerance $tol$, max iterations $max\_iter$
    \State \textbf{Output:} Iteration count $n$, root approximation $x$, function value $f(x)$, final interval $[a, b]$
    \State $f_a \gets f(a)$, $f_b \gets f(b)$, $\epsilon \gets 10^{-20}$
    \State \textit{// Check if either bound is a root}
    \If{$|f_a| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, a, f_a, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$|f_b| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, b, f_b, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$f_a \times f_b \geq 0$}
        \State \Return $\text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}$
    \EndIf
    \For{$n \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $max\_iter$}
        \State \textit{// Bisection Step}
        \State $mid \gets 0.5 \times (a + b)$
        \State $f_{mid} \gets f(mid)$
        \If{$|f_{mid}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, mid, f_{mid}, a, b$
        \EndIf
        \If{$f_a \times f_{mid} < 0$}
            \State $b, f_b \gets mid, f_{mid}$
        \Else
            \State $a, f_a \gets mid, f_{mid}$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// False Position Step with exception handling}
        \State $dx \gets (a \times f_b) - (b \times f_a)$
        \State \textbf{try:}
        \State \quad $fp \gets dx / (f_b - f_a)$
        \State \textbf{catch} (division by zero, overflow, value error):
        \State \quad $fp \gets dx / ((f_b - f_a) + \epsilon)$
        \State $f_{fp} \gets f(fp)$
        \If{$|f_{fp}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, fp, f_{fp}, a, b$
        \EndIf
        \If{$f_a \times f_{fp} < 0$}
            \State $b, f_b \gets fp, f_{fp}$
        \Else
            \State $a, f_a \gets fp, f_{fp}$
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State \textit{// Max iterations reached}
    \State $final\_x \gets 0.5 \times (a + b)$
    \State $f_{final\_x} \gets f(final\_x)$
    \State \Return $max\_iter, final\_x, f_{final\_x}, a, b$
    \end{algorithmic}

\subsubsection{Algorithm 2: Optimized Bisection-False Position with Modified Secant (Optimized\_BFMS)}

The Optimized Bisection-False Position with Modified Secant (Optimized\_BFMS) algorithm represents an advanced three-phase hybrid approach that builds upon the Optimized\_BF framework by incorporating a modified secant step for enhanced convergence acceleration. This algorithm addresses the limitations of traditional bracketing methods by introducing derivative-free accelerated interpolation techniques while maintaining the reliability guarantees of bracketing approaches.

The mathematical foundation of this algorithm extends the two-phase strategy of Optimized\_BF with a third interpolation phase. The first two phases (bisection and false position) provide the robust foundation, while the modified secant phase introduces a derivative-free acceleration step. The modified secant method approximates the derivative using finite differences:

\begin{equation}
f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x + \delta) - f(x)}{\delta}
\end{equation}

where $\delta = 10^{-4}$ is a carefully chosen perturbation parameter that balances numerical stability with approximation accuracy. This approximation enables the computation of a secant estimate:

\begin{equation}
x_S = x - \frac{f(x)}{f'(x)} \approx x - \frac{\delta \cdot f(x)}{f(x + \delta) - f(x)}
\end{equation}

The key innovation of this algorithm lies in its acceptance criterion for the secant estimate. The algorithm only accepts the secant estimate $x_S$ if it satisfies two conditions: (1) it lies within the current bracketing interval $[a, b]$, ensuring the bracketing property is maintained, and (2) it provides a better approximation than the false position estimate, i.e., $|f(x_S)| < |f(fp)|$. This conservative approach ensures that the algorithm never sacrifices reliability for speed.

The advantages of this three-phase approach are manifold. The bisection phase guarantees robust interval reduction to half, passing new intervals to the false position phase that provides accelerated convergence through linear interpolation. Then, the modified secant phase offers additional acceleration when the function output is better, without losing the bracketing guarantees. This combination results in superior convergence rates while maintaining the reliability of the bracketing methods.

The algorithm method signature is Optimized\_BFMS$(f, a, b, tol, max\_iter, \delta = 10^{-4})$, where $f$ is the target function, $a$ and $b$ define the initial interval $[a, b]$, $tol$ is the convergence tolerance, $max\_iter$ is the maximum iteration count, and $\delta$ is a small parameter defaulting to $10^{-4}$ for the secant approximation. It returns $(n, x, f_x, a_{final}, b_{final})$, with $n$ as the iteration count, $x$ as the root approximation, $f_x$ as the function value at $x$, and $a_{final}$ and $b_{final}$ as the final interval bounds.

The choice of $\delta = 10^{-4}$ is based on extensive numerical experimentation and represents an optimal balance between approximation accuracy and numerical stability. Smaller values of $\delta$ provide more accurate derivative approximations but may lead to numerical instability, while larger values ensure stability but may reduce the effectiveness of the secant acceleration.

\captionof{algorithm}{Pseudocode for the Hybrid Bisection-False Position-Modified Secant (HbisectionFalseMS) algorithm. This method sequentially applies a bisection, false position, and modified secant step in each iteration.}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input:} Function $f$, interval $[a, b]$, tolerance $tol$, max iterations $max\_iter$, perturbation $\delta$
    \State \textbf{Output:} Iteration count $n$, root approximation $x$, function value $f(x)$, final interval $[a, b]$
    \State $f_a \gets f(a)$, $f_b \gets f(b)$, $n \gets 0$, $\epsilon \gets 10^{-20}$
    \State \textit{// Check if either bound is a root}
    \If{$|f_a| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, a, f_a, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$|f_b| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, b, f_b, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$f_a \times f_b \geq 0$}
        \State \Return $\text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}$
    \EndIf
    \While{$n < max\_iter$}
        \State $n \gets n + 1$
        
        \State \textit{// 1. Bisection Step to narrow the interval}
        \State $x_B \gets (a + b) \times 0.5$
        \State $f_{x_B} \gets f(x_B)$
        \If{$f_a \times f_{x_B} < 0$}
            \State $b \gets x_B$, $f_b \gets f_{x_B}$
        \Else
            \State $a \gets x_B$, $f_a \gets f_{x_B}$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// 2. False Position Step with exception handling}
        \State $dx \gets (a \times f_b) - (b \times f_a)$
        \State \textbf{try:}
        \State \quad $x_F \gets dx / (f_b - f_a)$
        \State \textbf{catch} (division by zero, overflow, value error):
        \State \quad $x_F \gets dx / ((f_b - f_a) + \epsilon)$
        \State $f_{x_F} \gets f(x_F)$
        \If{$f_a \times f_{x_F} < 0$}
            \State $b \gets x_F$, $f_b \gets f_{x_F}$
        \Else
            \State $a \gets x_F$, $f_a \gets f_{x_F}$
        \EndIf
        \If{$|f_{x_F}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, x_F, f_{x_F}, a, b$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// 3. Modified Secant Step with exception handling}
        \State \textbf{try:}
        \State \quad $x_S \gets x_F - \delta \times f_{x_F} / (f(x_F + \delta) - f_{x_F})$
        \State \textbf{catch} (division by zero, overflow, value error):
        \State \quad $x_S \gets x_F - \delta \times f_{x_F} / ((f(x_F + \delta) - f_{x_F}) + \epsilon)$
        \If{$a < x_S < b$}
            \State $f_{x_S} \gets f(x_S)$
            \If{$|f_{x_S}| < |f_{x_F}|$}
                \If{$f_a \times f_{x_S} < 0$}
                    \State $b \gets x_S$, $f_b \gets f_{x_S}$
                \Else
                    \State $a \gets x_S$, $f_a \gets f_{x_S}$
                \EndIf
                \If{$|f_{x_S}| \leq tol$}
                    \State \Return $n, x_S, f_{x_S}, a, b$
                \EndIf
            \EndIf
        \EndIf
    \EndWhile
    \State \textit{// Max iterations reached, return the midpoint of the final interval}
    \State $x \gets (a + b) \times 0.5$
    \State $f_x \gets f(x)$
    \State \Return $n, x, f_x, a, b$
    \end{algorithmic}

\subsubsection{Algorithm 3: Optimized Trisection-False Position (Optimized\_TF)}

The Optimized Trisection-False Position (Optimized\_TF) algorithm represents an innovative hybrid approach that leverages the theoretical advantages of trisection over traditional bisection while incorporating the convergence acceleration of the false position method. This algorithm addresses the fundamental limitation of bisection methods, which reduce the interval size by a factor of only 2 in each iteration, by implementing a more aggressive three-way interval splitting strategy.

The mathematical foundation of this algorithm is based on the trisection method, which divides the interval $[a, b]$ into three equal subintervals by computing two interior points:

\begin{equation}
x_1 = a + \frac{(b - a)}{3} \quad \text{and} \quad x_2 = b - \frac{(b - a)}{3}
\end{equation}


This trisection approach provides a theoretical advantage over bisection by potentially reducing the interval size more rapidly. While bisection has a reduction factor of $\frac{1}{2}$ per iteration, trisection can achieve a reduction factor of $\frac{1}{3}$ in the worst case, and potentially better in favorable scenarios where the root lies in the middle subinterval, as analyzed by \cite{demir2008trisection}.

The algorithm has two sequential phases at each iteration. The first one utilizes trisection to identify the subinterval containing the root by evaluating $f(x_1)$ and $f(x_2)$ and picking the subinterval that changes sign. The second applies the false position method to the identified subinterval, providing accelerated convergence through linear interpolation.

The key advantage of this combined hybrid approach is its potential for faster interval reduction compared to traditional bisection-based methods. The trisection phase can reduce the interval size by up to a factor of 3 in a single iteration, while the false position phase provides additional convergence acceleration within the reduced interval.

The Algorithm is defined with the method signature Optimized\_TF$(f, a, b, tol, max\_iter)$, where $f$ is the function to evaluate, $a$ and $b$ define the initial interval $[a, b]$ containing the root, $tol$ is the convergence tolerance, and $max\_iter$ is the maximum number of iterations. It returns $(n, x, f_x, a_{final}, b_{final})$, with $n$ as the iteration count, $x$ as the root approximation, $f_x$ as the function value at $x$, and $a_{final}$ and $b_{final}$ as the final interval bounds.

The algorithm incorporates robust error handling for division by zero scenarios in the false position step. The implementation maintains the bracketing property throughout the computation, guaranteeing convergence.

\captionof{algorithm}{Pseudocode for the Optimized Trisection-False Position (Optimized\_TF) algorithm. This method leverages trisection for rapid interval reduction and false position for convergence acceleration.}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input:} Function $f$, interval $[a, b]$, tolerance $tol$, max iterations $max\_iter$
    \State \textbf{Output:} Root approximation $x$, function value $f(x)$, final interval $[a, b]$
    \State $f_a \gets f(a)$, $f_b \gets f(b)$, $\epsilon \gets 10^{-20}$
    \State \textit{// Check if either bound is a root}
    \If{$|f_a| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, a, f_a, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$|f_b| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, b, f_b, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \For{$n \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $max\_iter$}
        \State \textit{// 1. Trisection Step}
        \State $diff \gets b - a$
        \State $x_1 \gets a + diff/3$
        \State $x_2 \gets b - diff/3$
        \State $f_{x_1} \gets f(x_1)$, $f_{x_2} \gets f(x_2)$
        \If{$|f_{x_1}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, x_1, f_{x_1}, a, b$
        \EndIf
        \If{$|f_{x_2}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, x_2, f_{x_2}, a, b$
        \EndIf
        
        \State \textit{// Update interval based on trisection}
        \If{$f_a \times f_{x_1} < 0$}
            \State $a, b, f_b \gets a, x_1, f_{x_1}$
        \ElsIf{$f_{x_1} \times f_{x_2} < 0$}
            \State $a, b, f_a, f_b \gets x_1, x_2, f_{x_1}, f_{x_2}$
        \Else
            \State $a, f_a \gets x_2, f_{x_2}$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// 2. False Position Step with exception handling}
        \State $dx \gets (a \times f_b) - (b \times f_a)$
        \State $dd \gets f_b - f_a$
        \State \textbf{try:}
        \State \quad $x_F \gets dx / dd$
        \State \textbf{catch} (division by zero, overflow, value error):
        \State \quad $x_F \gets dx / (dd + \epsilon)$
        \State $f_{x_F} \gets f(x_F)$
        \If{$|f_{x_F}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, x_F, f_{x_F}, a, b$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// Update interval based on false position}
        \If{$f_a \times f_{x_F} < 0$}
            \State $b, f_b \gets x_F, f_{x_F}$
        \Else
            \State $a, f_a \gets x_F, f_{x_F}$
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State \textit{// Max iterations reached}
    \State $x \gets (a+b)/2$
    \State $f_x \gets f(x)$
    \State \Return $max\_iter, x, f_x, a, b$
    \end{algorithmic}

\subsubsection{Algorithm 4: Optimized Trisection-False Position with Modified Secant (Optimized\_TFMS)}

The Optimized Trisection-False Position with Modified Secant (Optimized\_TFMS) algorithm represents the most sophisticated of the four proposed hybrid methods, combining the aggressive interval reduction of trisection with the convergence acceleration of both false position and modified secant techniques. This algorithm addresses the challenge of achieving maximum convergence speed while maintaining the reliability guarantees of bracketing methods through a carefully orchestrated three-phase strategy.

The mathematical foundation of this algorithm integrates three complementary numerical techniques in a hierarchical manner. The first phase employs trisection to rapidly reduce the search interval by computing two interior points:

\begin{equation}
x_1 = a + \frac{b - a}{3} \quad \text{and} \quad x_2 = b - \frac{b - a}{3}
\end{equation}

This trisection phase provides the most aggressive interval reduction among all proposed algorithms, potentially reducing the interval size by a factor of 3 in a single iteration. The second phase applies the false position method to the identified subinterval, providing accelerated convergence through linear interpolation:

\begin{equation}
fp = \frac{a \cdot f(b) - b \cdot f(a)}{f(b) - f(a)}
\end{equation}

The third phase introduces a modified secant step for additional acceleration, using finite difference approximation:

\begin{equation}
x_S = fp - \frac{\delta \cdot f(fp)}{f(fp + \delta) - f(fp)}
\end{equation}

where $\delta = 10^{-4}$ is the perturbation parameter for derivative approximation.

The key innovation of this algorithm lies in its acceptance criteria for the modified secant estimate. The algorithm only accepts it's output $x_S$ if it satisfies three conditions: (1) it lies within the current bracketing interval $[a, b]$, ensuring root bracketing is maintained, (2) it provides a better approximation than the false position output, i.e., $|f(x_S)| < |f(fp)|$, and (3) the function evaluation at the secant estimate is numerically stable. This multi-level validation ensures that the algorithm never sacrifices reliability for speed.

The advantages of this three-phase approach are substantial. The trisection phase provides the fastest possible interval reduction, the false position phase offers reliable convergence acceleration, and the modified secant phase provides additional acceleration when conditions are favorable. This combination results in the lowest average iteration count among all proposed algorithms while maintaining robust convergence guarantees.

The algorithm is defined with the method signature Optimized\_TFMS$(f, a, b, tol, max\_iter, \delta = 10^{-4})$, where $f$ is the function, $a$ and $b$ define the initial interval $[a, b]$, $tol$ is the tolerance, $max\_iter$ is the maximum iteration count, and $\delta$ is a small perturbation value defaulting to $10^{-4}$ for the secant step. It returns $(n, x, f_x, a_{final}, b_{final})$, with $n$ as the iteration count, $x$ as the root approximation, $f_x$ as the function value at $x$, and $a_{final}$ and $b_{final}$ as the final interval bounds.

This algorithm represents the pinnacle of the hybrid approach, demonstrating that sophisticated combinations of numerical techniques can achieve superior performance without compromising reliability. The experimental results confirm that this three-phase strategy provides the best overall performance in terms of both iteration count and computational efficiency across the diverse test suite.

\captionof{algorithm}{Pseudocode for the Optimized Trisection-False Position with Modified Secant (Optimized\_TFMS) algorithm. This advanced hybrid combines trisection, false position, and a modified secant step for maximum efficiency.}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input:} Function $f$, interval $[a, b]$, tolerance $tol$, max iterations $max\_iter$, $\delta = 10^{-4}$
    \State \textbf{Output:} Root approximation $x$, function value $f(x)$, final interval $[a, b]$
    \State $f_a \gets f(a)$, $f_b \gets f(b)$, $\epsilon \gets 10^{-20}$
    \State \textit{// Check if either bound is a root}
    \If{$|f_a| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, a, f_a, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$|f_b| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, b, f_b, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$f_a \times f_b \geq 0$}
        \State \Return $\text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}$
    \EndIf
    \For{$n \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $max\_iter$}
        \State \textit{// 1. Trisection Step}
        \State $diff \gets b - a$
        \State $x_1 \gets a + diff/3$, $x_2 \gets b - diff/3$
        \State $f_{x_1} \gets f(x_1)$, $f_{x_2} \gets f(x_2)$
        \If{$|f_{x_1}| \leq tol$} \State \Return $n, x_1, f_{x_1}, a, b$ \EndIf
        \If{$|f_{x_2}| \leq tol$} \State \Return $n, x_2, f_{x_2}, a, b$ \EndIf
        
        \State \textit{// Update interval based on trisection results}
        \If{$f_a \times f_{x_1} < 0$}
            \State $b, f_b \gets x_1, f_{x_1}$
        \ElsIf{$f_{x_1} \times f_{x_2} < 0$}
            \State $a, b, f_a, f_b \gets x_1, x_2, f_{x_1}, f_{x_2}$
        \Else
            \State $a, f_a \gets x_2, f_{x_2}$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// 2. False Position Step with exception handling}
        \State $dx \gets (a \times f_b) - (b \times f_a)$
        \State \textbf{try:}
        \State \quad $x_F \gets dx / (f_b - f_a)$
        \State \textbf{catch} (division by zero, overflow, value error):
        \State \quad $x_F \gets dx / ((f_b - f_a) + \epsilon)$
        \State $f_{x_F} \gets f(x_F)$
        \If{$f_a \times f_{x_F} < 0$}
            \State $b, f_b \gets x_F, f_{x_F}$
        \Else
            \State $a, f_a \gets x_F, f_{x_F}$
        \EndIf
        \If{$|f_{x_F}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, x_F, f_{x_F}, a, b$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// 3. Modified Secant Step with exception handling}
        \State \textbf{try:}
        \State \quad $x_S \gets x_F - \delta \times f_{x_F} / (f(x_F + \delta) - f_{x_F})$
        \State \textbf{catch} (division by zero, overflow, value error):
        \State \quad $x_S \gets x_F - \delta \times f_{x_F} / ((f(x_F + \delta) - f_{x_F}) + \epsilon)$
        \If{$a < x_S < b$}
            \State $f_{x_S} \gets f(x_S)$
            \If{$|f_{x_S}| < |f_{x_F}|$}
                \If{$f_a \times f_{x_S} < 0$}
                    \State $b, f_b \gets x_S, f_{x_S}$
                \Else
                    \State $a, f_a \gets x_S, f_{x_S}$
                \EndIf
                \If{$|f_{x_S}| \leq tol$}
                    \State \Return $n, x_S, f_{x_S}, a, b$
                \EndIf
            \EndIf
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State \textit{// Max iterations reached}
    \State $x \gets (a+b) \times 0.5$
    \State $f_x \gets f(x)$
    \State \Return $max\_iter, x, f_x, a, b$
    \end{algorithmic}

\section{Theoretical Analysis}\label{sec:theoretical_analysis}

A primary contribution of this work is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed hybrid algorithms. While the Intermediate Value Theorem guarantees their eventual convergence, a specific analysis of the multi-phase structure is required to establish the order of convergence and formally characterize the performance bounds.

\subsection{Order of Convergence}

Let $\alpha$ be a simple root of $f(x)$, where $f''$ is continuous in the neighborhood of $\alpha$, and let $e_n = x_n - \alpha$ be the error of an iterate $x_n$. The order of convergence is $p$ if $|e_{n+1}| \leq C |e_n|^p$ for some constant $C > 0$ as $n \to \infty$.

\subsubsection{Analysis of Optimized\_BF and Optimized\_TF}

The convergence rate of these two-phase algorithms is governed by the False Position step. For a sequence of iterates generated by False Position, the error recurrence leads to a well-established superlinear order of convergence of \textbf{$p \approx 1.618$}. The asymptotic error constant depends on the function's derivatives at the root, with the error relation being $|e_{n+1}| \approx C |e_n| |e_{n-1}|$, where $C = |\frac{f''(\alpha)}{2f'(\alpha)}|$. Our hybrid structure improves upon the classic method by using the bracketing step (Bisection or Trisection) to ensure the interval reliably shrinks, preventing the stagnation that can occur in pure False Position and ensuring this superlinear rate is reliably achieved.

\subsubsection{Analysis of Optimized\_BFMS and Optimized\_TFMS}

These three-phase algorithms incorporate a Modified Secant step, an approximation of the quadratically convergent Newton's method. The error relation for Newton's method is given by $e_{n+1} = C e_n^2 + O(e_n^3)$. Our finite difference approximation of the derivative introduces a perturbation, as shown by its Taylor expansion:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{f(x_n+\delta) - f(x_n)}{\delta} = f'(x_n) + \frac{\delta}{2}f''(x_n) + O(\delta^2)
\end{equation}
The update rule is therefore a perturbed Newton step. Standard theorems on such methods confirm that a fixed perturbation of order $O(\delta)$ in the derivative approximation leads to linear convergence in the terminal phase \cite[e.g.,][]{kelley1995iterative}. However, the practical power of the step is best understood by analyzing its performance bounds within our hybrid structure.

\textbf{Best-Case Performance (Near-Quadratic Acceleration):} When the Modified Secant step is consistently accepted, it becomes the primary driver of convergence. Its error recurrence can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    e_{n,S} \approx \frac{f''(\alpha)}{2f'(\alpha)} e_{n,F}^2 - \frac{\delta}{2} \frac{f''(\alpha)}{f'(\alpha)} e_{n,F}
\end{equation}
This relation formally shows that the convergence is driven by a powerful quadratic term but is ultimately limited by a linear error term proportional to $\delta$, resulting in the "effectively quadratic" behavior observed empirically.

\textbf{Worst-Case Performance (Graceful Degradation):} We now provide a brief proof sketch for the algorithm's performance when the Modified Secant step is rejected.

\textit{Proof Sketch.} Let the sequence of iterates generated by the full hybrid algorithm be $\{x_n\}$ and the sequence generated by its internal False Position steps be $\{y_n\}$. The convergence order of $\{y_n\}$ is $p_{FP} \approx 1.618$. By the algorithm's design, the Modified Secant estimate $x_S$ is only accepted if $|f(x_S)| < |f(y_n)|$. If it is rejected, the next state is determined by $y_n$. Therefore, for all $n$, the error of the accepted iterate satisfies $|f(x_{n+1})| \leq |f(y_n)|$. Since $f(x) \approx f'(\alpha)(x-\alpha)$ near the root, it follows that the error sequence $|e_n|$ of the hybrid algorithm converges at least as fast as the error sequence of the underlying False Position method. Thus, the order of convergence $p$ of the hybrid algorithm is bounded below by that of False Position: $p \ge p_{FP} \approx 1.618$. This guarantees at least superlinear convergence.

\textbf{Conclusion on Convergence:} The practical convergence of BFMS and TFMS is an adaptive process that opportunistically leverages near-quadratic steps, while the architecture provides a formal guarantee of graceful degradation to a robust superlinear lower bound. This property is a key feature of our multi-phase design.
\subsection{Finite-Precision Considerations and Numerical Stability}

The primary source of instability in these methods is the denominator of the interpolation formulas. For both the false position and modified secant steps, these take the form $f(y) - f(z)$ for two points $y$ and $z$. In standard floating-point arithmetic (IEEE 754 double precision), when $y$ and $z$ are very close to each other, their function values $f(y)$ and $f(z)$ will also be very close, so when perform The subtraction of these two nearly equal numbers, it can lead to \textbf{catastrophic cancellation}, a phenomenon where a large portion of the significant digits are lost, resulting in a denominator dominated by relative error. Our pseudocode's inclusion of a small machine epsilon ($\epsilon=10^{-20}$) is a pragmatic heuristic to prevent division by zero but does not resolve the underlying issue of precision loss from cancellation.

Furthermore, the choice of the perturbation parameter $\delta$ in the modified secant method is critical and represents a fundamental trade-off between both truncation and round-off errors.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Truncation Error:} If $\delta$ is too large, the finite difference $\frac{f(x+\delta) - f(x)}{\delta}$ is a poor approximation of the derivative $f'(x)$, and the method's convergence slows. This is a truncation error, as we are truncating the Taylor series expansion.
    \item \textbf{Round-off Error:} If $\delta$ is too small, it becomes comparable to the machine precision. The term $f(x+\delta)$ will be very close to $f(x)$, and their subtraction will suffer from the catastrophic cancellation described above. This is a round-off error.
\end{itemize}
An optimal choice for $\delta$ balances these two error sources, and a common heuristic is $\delta \approx \sqrt{\epsilon_{\text{mach}}} \cdot |x|$, where $\epsilon_{\text{mach}}$ is the machine epsilon (approx. $10^{-16}$ for double precision). Our fixed choice of $\delta = 10^{-4}$ is a robust, general-purpose value for functions where the root is of order unity, but an adaptive $\delta$ could provide better performance and stability, representing a direction for future research. The impact of these finite-precision effects is empirically evaluated in Section~\ref{sec:experimental}.

\subsection{SymPy Implementation Details}

All algorithms developed are implemented using the SymPy library for symbolic computations, providing a number of important advantages for the numerical analysis research. Some major functions of the library were exercised in the implementation:

\textbf{Symbolic Function Definition:} The functions are defined using SymPy symbols, thereby allowing an exact mathematical characterization with automatic differential capabilities. This procedure assures that the mathematical formulations are used solely in the form defined, unhampered by any numerical errors that may have crept in due to finite difference approximations.

\textbf{High-Precision Arithmetic:} SymPy provides arbitrary-precision arithmetic, making it an accurate requirement for measuring error in iterative numerical methods. In this respect, this property is quite relevant for the high-precision tolerance of $10^{-14}$ adopted in the present study.

\textbf{Automatic Differentiation:} The methods requiring derivative information can avail themselves of SymPy's automatic differentiation, which returns exact derivatives without succumbing to finite difference approximations, thus guaranteeing maximum accuracy.

\textbf{Comprehensive Error Handling:} The implementation features extensive error surveillances for rare cases like division by zero, overflow conditions, and convergence failures.

\textbf{Experimental Framework} Such a heavy experimental framework is in place for storing and analyzing results, that SQLite is assuredly a good choice for efficient data management. The inner loop contains 100 runs of each algorithm, which are executed then 100 times in the outer loop to provide statistically valid measurements of their performance. The drawback of going loudly nested is that it would provide results most probably free of system noise and strong enough statistically for possibly useful comparisons. 

\subsection{Performance metrics}

The algorithms' performance was discriminated using four measuring parameters that relate efficiency and accuracy to give a deep conviction:

\textbf{Number of Iterations}: This defines the rate of convergence of all algorithms to the root; with fewer iterations generally preferring an algorithm, but this has to be traded for the computation cost per iteration.

\textbf{CPU Times}: On the basis of the number of iterations and their computational costs, these measure the actual computational efficiency. CPU time remains the most viable measure when referring to applications in the real world where resource consumption matters.

\textbf{Function Value at Computed Root:} The accuracy of the root approximation is basically measured by this metric - how much close the computed value is to a true root of the function. A number very close to zero confirms maximum accuracy. 

\textbf{Final Interval Size:} This metric measures how precise the root approximation is, with respect to the actual size of the interval where the computed root lies in. Smaller intervals indicate higher precision.

Convergence tolerance was kept at $10^{-14}$ to ensure very precise results, and each algorithm was terminated after reaching the limit of 10,000 iterations to avoid loop problems within the algorithm. These parameters were chosen according to the features and needs of high-precision numerical analysis as well as the capabilities of the SymPy library.

\subsection{CPU Time Measurement}

The CPU time for each one of the algorithms was measured using Python's \texttt{time.perf\_counter()} function, which gives high-resolution timing with nanosecond precision. This timing function is designed specifically for performance measurement and is unaffected by other system-level timing problems or adjustments in the system clock.

The procedure for a given test problem is to run the algorithm 100 times in a nested loop and to repeat it outside the loop for another 100 times. Once completed, that total elapsed time will be recorded as well as averaged to get a robust measure of computational efficiency. This method reduces system noise including garbage collection, context switching, and more operating system activities that could happen to timing measurements.

For it guarantees that the results are not consequences of transient conditions of the system, but rather representative of true performance of the algorithm; the 100 times internally will make it possible to distinguish meaningful differences by providing enough statistical power. The measurement repetition is sufficient to guarantee stable and reproducible results.

This is the method that is followed by best practices for recent usages in hybrid algorithms literature \cite{sabharwal2019blended, badr2022novel}, laying a strong foundation for comparative performance evaluation.

The experimental setting therefore has the same computational environment and the same settings for precision and measurement methods across all algorithms. Such stringency provides very good comparative performance data for the hybrid algorithms proposed and affords valid conclusions to be made regarding their relative performance.

Statistical and Significance Analysis

The design for the experiment carried out here was a highly repeated 100 times repeat. This will yield robust statistics for performance analysis. As expected, the coefficient of variation (CV) of CPU time measurements tends to be low (e.g., for Opt.BFMS: $\text{CV} = \frac{\text{std dev}}{\text{mean}} = \frac{0.00100}{0.00109} \approx 0.92$), while for more stable methods, it is actually much lower, indicating really good measurements.

The performance gains offered by the hybrid algorithms are statistically significant. A paired t-test against the CPU times of best hybrid algorithm, \textbf{Optimized BFMS} (mean $=0.00109$), versus a traditional algorithm, \textbf{Bisection} (mean $=0.00577$), yields a computed p-value of less than 0.001 ($n=42$, pairs, $t = \frac{\bar{d}}{s_d/\sqrt{n}}$), thus confirming the significance of the performance improvement statistically.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

It was by experimentation as well as theory that the work proved that these multi-phase jointly hybrid algorithms that work one after the other or sequentially have a winning combination in speed, efficiency, and robustness. The first-ranked algorithm, namely \textbf{Optimized\_BFMS}, proved to have the least average CPU time along with the very minimum average NFE values, confirming the effectiveness of the algorithm in terms of improvement of performance being grounded on algorithm efficiency. Interpretation of these findings with respect to current methods, as well as a look at other broader impacting issues, traditionally follows.

Three phases structure maximized the success of Optimized\_BFMS. The first measure is a bisection that guarantees interval reduction: thus, a solid foundation for the subsequent better speedup steps. Then, a false position and modified secant steps permit powerful superlinear convergence to quickly sharpen the location of the root. Empirical data indicate that the overheads of doing these three different steps each iteration more than compensate for the striking reduction in the total number of iterations required for convergence.

The trisection-based algorithms (Optimized\_TF and Optimized\_TFMS) were relatively efficient in terms of NFE. For instance, among all algorithms, Optimized\_TFMS turned out to be the second-most efficient algorithm. However, their somewhat longer CPU times compared to the bisection-based counterparts iowy suggest that, while the time taken by evaluating two interior points for trisection can outweigh the gain from more aggressive interval reductions at times.

The experimental framework presented reliable and reproducible results thanks to the combination of SymPy for symbolic computation and SQLite for data management. It was found that for every algorithm, 100 repetitions of the experiment made the results statistically valid, whereas tolerance of very high precision equal to $10^{-14}$ ensured root approximations to be quite accurate. The all-embracing test suite has transcendental, polynomial, trigonometric, logarithmic, and exponential functions included, thereby demonstrating the versatility of the algorithms across mathematics.

Theoretical support for such hybrid methods is now provided by the latest advances of numerical analysis. The study develops the framework built by \cite{sabharwal2019blended} and \cite{badr2022novel} proofs of superlinear convergence without compromising the reliability of bracketing ways accomplished in their hybrid algorithms. The modified secant technique is followed along the lines set forth by \cite{mathews2004numerical}. The modeling of the trisection method is drawn from the theoretical analysis done by \cite{demir2008trisection}.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This document presents four new hybrid algorithms bracketing roots. In these concepts, the bracketing algorithms are then combined with traditional bracketing methods with exceleration in interpolation techniques. All the hybrid algorithms enter upon more accelerated convergence over traditional methods, out of which Optimized\_BFMS stands first out of all four. The algorithms were achieved equally in all test functions. It also shows a significant improvement of CPU time over classical algorithms. Clearly, these results point out the efficiency of multi-phase hybrid methods in root finding numerically and provide an encouragement for immediate use in many disciplines-science and engineering-related problems.

Their contributions to theory involve the development of four novel, distinct hybrid algorithms that integrate bracketing techniques and open ones, while ensuring their convergence, just that it is faster. The experimental analysis, with its large diversity of 42 test functions and a very rigid high-precisions framework, attested the statistical significance of the results and their robustness. 

The algorithms are competitive against a wide spectrum of mathematical test problems, but there is still a recommendation to test them even in a real-world engineering and physics application case for establishing generality. Future works will include expanding the testing suite by adding real-life case studies and going beyond in hybridization strategies.
