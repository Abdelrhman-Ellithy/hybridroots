\title{Multi-Phase Hybrid Algorithms for Numerical Root Finding \\ \large Four New Hybrid Root Bracketing Algorithms}

\author{Abdelrahman Ellithy$^{1}$}
\affiliation{Department of Scientific Computing, Faculty of Computers and Artificial Intelligence, Benha University, Benha, Egypt}

\date{\today}


\begin{abstract}
Finding roots of nonlinear equations requires balancing the guaranteed convergence of traditional bracketing methods with the speed of accelerated techniques. This paper introduces four novel hybrid algorithms: \textbf{Optimized Bisection-False Position (Optimized\_BF)}, \textbf{Optimized Bisection-False Position-Modified Secant (Optimized\_BFMS)}, \textbf{Optimized Trisection-False Position (Optimized\_TF)}, and \textbf{Optimized Trisection-False Position-Modified Secant (Optimized\_TFMS)} that synergistically combine reliable interval reduction with accelerated interpolation steps. We provide a theoretical analysis of their convergence properties and validate their performance on a comprehensive benchmark of 42 diverse problems, including polynomial, transcendental, and ill-conditioned functions. A rigorous comparative analysis is conducted against traditional baselines (Bisection, False Position) and established state-of-the-art methods (Brent's method, Quadratic Interval Refinement). Our results show that the premier algorithm, \textbf{Optimized\_BFMS}, achieves the best overall performance, winning on \textbf{47.6\%} of problems and demonstrating significant speedups over both the fastest traditional baseline (Bisection, \textbf{5.3$\times$}) and state-of-the-art algorithms like Brent's method (\textbf{1.7$\times$}), and QIR method (\textbf{2.2$\times$}) on average across our benchmark suite. Our findings establish these hybrid approaches as robust, efficient, and theoretically-grounded alternatives for numerical root finding.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Root finding is a fundamental problem in numerical analysis with applications spanning engineering, physics, economics, and computer science. Traditional root bracketing methods, such as Bisection and Regula Falsi (False Position), provide guaranteed convergence but often suffer from slow convergence rates. This reliability-versus-speed trade-off has motivated the development of hybrid approaches that aim to retain the robustness of bracketing while accelerating performance using multi-phase accelerated interpolation techniques, a strategy that has shown significant promise in prior work \cite{sabharwal2019blended, badr2022novel}.

The landscape of root-finding algorithms has evolved beyond these classical methods. Modern, state-of-the-art algorithms like Quadratic Interval Refinement (QIR) \cite{abbott2014quadratic} and the widely-deployed Brent's method \cite{brent1973algorithms} are now standard benchmarks for high performance. While these methods are highly effective, there remains an opportunity to develop new algorithms that are either faster or exhibit more consistent performance across a wider range of challenging functions. This paper addresses that gap by introducing four new hybrid algorithms and evaluating them not only against traditional baselines but also against these established, high-performance algorithms.

The main contributions of this paper are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The design and implementation of \textbf{four novel hybrid algorithms} that blend reliable interval reduction (Bisection and Trisection) with accelerated interpolation and quasi-Newton steps.
    \item A \textbf{rigorous theoretical analysis} of the convergence order and error bounds for each proposed algorithm.
    \item A \textbf{comprehensive performance evaluation} on a diverse benchmark of 42 test functions, augmented with notoriously difficult cases to test robustness and numerical stability.
    \item A \textbf{comparative analysis} against both traditional baselines and modern state-of-the-art methods, using metrics that include function evaluations and performance in finite-precision arithmetic.
    \item The empirical finding that our premier hybrid algorithm, \textbf{Optimized\_BFMS}, provides a superior balance of speed and reliability, demonstrably outperforming the established benchmarks on average.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work and Context}\label{sec:related_work}

The concept of hybridizing root-finding methods to achieve both reliability and speed is well-established, dating back to the influential algorithms of the 1970s. Dekker's algorithm (1969) was a seminal contribution, combining the safety of bisection with the speed of the secant method. It introduced the now-common strategy of falling back on a bisection step if the faster interpolation step fails to make sufficient progress. Brent's method \cite{brent1973algorithms} refined this idea by replacing the secant method with inverse quadratic interpolation, achieving a higher order of convergence while retaining the bisection safeguard, making it one of the most widely used and robust root-finders today.

Our work builds upon this tradition but explores a different structural approach. Whereas Dekker's and Brent's methods use bisection as a conditional fallback, our algorithms employ a deterministic, multi-phase sequence within each iteration. For example, the BFMS algorithm systematically applies a bisection, a false position, and a modified secant step, leveraging the output of each to inform the next. This structured approach avoids the complex acceptance criteria of classic hybrids and aims to maximize progress in every iteration.

More recently, Quadratic Interval Refinement (QIR) \cite{abbott2012quadratic, abbott2014quadratic} has been proposed as a method that guarantees quadratic convergence for simple roots by fitting a quadratic model to three points to refine the root's location. While powerful, its performance can depend on the function's local behavior. The algorithms presented in this paper are differentiated by their reliance on simpler linear interpolation and finite-difference steps, which, as our experiments show, can be more efficient on average across a wider variety of function types.

Furthermore, this work is distinct from other recent hybrid methods included in our benchmark, such as those by Sabharwal (2019) \cite{sabharwal2019blended} and Badr (2021) \cite{badr2021comparative}. Those methods typically employ a "blended" strategy, often involving a conditional choice between, for example, a bisection or a false position update within an iteration. Our proposed algorithms differ fundamentally in their deterministic, \textbf{sequential multi-phase architecture}. For instance, our Optimized\_BF algorithm does not choose between bisection and false position; it executes a bisection step and then a false position step within the newly narrowed interval in every single iteration. This fixed sequential process is a unique architectural choice designed to enforce progress while consistently applying interpolation-based acceleration.

\section{Algorithm Design and Methodology}\label{sec:methodology}

This section details the design and implementation of the four novel hybrid algorithms. The core principle underlying each method is a deterministic, sequential multi-phase architecture executed within every iteration. This structure is designed to enforce guaranteed progress while systematically applying accelerated interpolation techniques. The algorithms integrate one of two reliable interval-reduction steps (Bisection or Trisection) with one or two acceleration steps (False Position and a Modified Secant). The specific rationale, mathematical formulation, and pseudocode for each of the four algorithms are presented in the following subsections.

\subsection{Algorithm Design, Hybrid Techniques and Pseudocode for Proposed Algorithms}
\subsubsection{Algorithm 1: Optimized Bisection-False Position (Optimized\_BF)}

The Optimized Bisection-False Position (Optimized\_BF) algorithm represents a sophisticated hybrid root-finding approach that strategically combines the guaranteed convergence of the bisection method with the accelerated convergence potential of the false position (regula falsi) method. This algorithm addresses the fundamental trade-off between reliability and speed in numerical root finding by employing a two-phase strategy within each iteration.

The mathematical foundation of this algorithm lies in the sequential application of two complementary techniques. The bisection phase provides a conservative but reliable interval reduction by computing the midpoint $mid = \frac{a + b}{2}$ and evaluating $f(mid)$. This step ensures that the root remains bracketed while providing a baseline estimate. The subsequent false position phase leverages linear interpolation between the interval endpoints to compute a potentially more accurate estimate using the formula:

\begin{equation}
fp = \frac{a \cdot f(b) - b \cdot f(a)}{f(b) - f(a)}
\end{equation}

This interpolation-based estimate often provides a better approximation to the root than the simple midpoint, especially for functions with significant curvature. The algorithm maintains the bracketing property by ensuring that the root remains contained within the updated interval $[a, b]$ after each iteration.

The key advantage of this hybrid approach is its robustness: even if the false position step produces an estimate outside the current interval (which can occur for certain function behaviors), the bisection step ensures that the interval is always properly reduced. This combination provides faster convergence than pure bisection while maintaining the reliability guarantees that make bracketing methods attractive for critical applications.

The algorithm is defined with the method signature Optimized\_BF$(f, a, b, tol, max\_iter)$, where $f$ is the function to be evaluated, $a$ and $b$ specify the initial interval $[a, b]$ containing a root (assumed to have opposite signs for $f(a)$ and $f(b)$), $tol$ sets the tolerance for convergence, and $max\_iter$ limits the number of iterations. It returns a tuple $(n, x, f_x, a_{final}, b_{final})$, where $n$ indicates the iteration count, $x$ is the root approximation, $f_x$ is the function value at $x$, and $a_{final}$ and $b_{final}$ are the final interval bounds.

The implementation uses SymPy for symbolic computation and high-precision arithmetic, with a tolerance of $10^{-14}$ for convergence testing. The algorithm incorporates robust error handling for potential division by zero in the false position step, ensuring stable operation across diverse function types.


\captionof{algorithm}{Pseudocode for the Optimized Bisection-False Position (Optimized\_BF) algorithm. This hybrid method combines the reliability of bisection with the accelerated convergence of the false position method.}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input:} Function $f$, interval $[a, b]$, tolerance $tol$, max iterations $max\_iter$
    \State \textbf{Output:} Iteration count $n$, root approximation $x$, function value $f(x)$, final interval $[a, b]$
    \State $f_a \gets f(a)$, $f_b \gets f(b)$, $\epsilon \gets 10^{-20}$
    \State \textit{// Check if either bound is a root}
    \If{$|f_a| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, a, f_a, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$|f_b| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, b, f_b, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$f_a \times f_b \geq 0$}
        \State \Return $\text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}$
    \EndIf
    \For{$n \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $max\_iter$}
        \State \textit{// Bisection Step}
        \State $mid \gets 0.5 \times (a + b)$
        \State $f_{mid} \gets f(mid)$
        \If{$|f_{mid}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, mid, f_{mid}, a, b$
        \EndIf
        \If{$f_a \times f_{mid} < 0$}
            \State $b, f_b \gets mid, f_{mid}$
        \Else
            \State $a, f_a \gets mid, f_{mid}$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// False Position Step with exception handling}
        \State $dx \gets (a \times f_b) - (b \times f_a)$
        \State \textbf{try:}
        \State \quad $fp \gets dx / (f_b - f_a)$
        \State \textbf{catch} (division by zero, overflow, value error):
        \State \quad $fp \gets dx / ((f_b - f_a) + \epsilon)$
        \State $f_{fp} \gets f(fp)$
        \If{$|f_{fp}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, fp, f_{fp}, a, b$
        \EndIf
        \If{$f_a \times f_{fp} < 0$}
            \State $b, f_b \gets fp, f_{fp}$
        \Else
            \State $a, f_a \gets fp, f_{fp}$
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State \textit{// Max iterations reached}
    \State $final\_x \gets 0.5 \times (a + b)$
    \State $f_{final\_x} \gets f(final\_x)$
    \State \Return $max\_iter, final\_x, f_{final\_x}, a, b$
    \end{algorithmic}

\subsubsection{Algorithm 2: Optimized Bisection-False Position with Modified Secant (Optimized\_BFMS)}

The Optimized Bisection-False Position with Modified Secant (Optimized\_BFMS) algorithm represents an advanced three-phase hybrid approach that builds upon the Optimized\_BF framework by incorporating a modified secant step for enhanced convergence acceleration. This algorithm addresses the limitations of traditional bracketing methods by introducing derivative-free accelerated interpolation techniques while maintaining the reliability guarantees of bracketing approaches.

The mathematical foundation of this algorithm extends the two-phase strategy of Optimized\_BF with a third interpolation phase. The first two phases (bisection and false position) provide the robust foundation, while the modified secant phase introduces a derivative-free acceleration step. The modified secant method approximates the derivative using finite differences:

\begin{equation}
f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x + \delta) - f(x)}{\delta}
\end{equation}

where $\delta = 10^{-4}$ is a carefully chosen perturbation parameter that balances numerical stability with approximation accuracy. This approximation enables the computation of a secant estimate:

\begin{equation}
x_S = x - \frac{f(x)}{f'(x)} \approx x - \frac{\delta \cdot f(x)}{f(x + \delta) - f(x)}
\end{equation}

The key innovation of this algorithm lies in its intelligent acceptance criterion for the secant estimate. The algorithm only accepts the secant estimate $x_S$ if it satisfies two conditions: (1) it lies within the current bracketing interval $[a, b]$, ensuring the bracketing property is maintained, and (2) it provides a better approximation than the false position estimate, i.e., $|f(x_S)| < |f(fp)|$. This conservative approach ensures that the algorithm never sacrifices reliability for speed.

The advantages of this three-phase approach are manifold. First, the bisection phase guarantees robust interval reduction. Second, the false position phase provides accelerated convergence through linear interpolation. Third, the modified secant phase offers additional acceleration when the function behavior is favorable, without compromising the bracketing guarantees. This combination results in superior convergence rates while maintaining the reliability that makes bracketing methods essential for critical applications.

The algorithm is defined with the method signature Optimized\_BFMS$(f, a, b, tol, max\_iter, \delta = 10^{-4})$, where $f$ is the target function, $a$ and $b$ define the initial interval $[a, b]$, $tol$ is the convergence tolerance, $max\_iter$ is the maximum iteration count, and $\delta$ is a small parameter defaulting to $10^{-4}$ for the secant approximation. It returns $(n, x, f_x, a_{final}, b_{final})$, with $n$ as the iteration count, $x$ as the root approximation, $f_x$ as the function value at $x$, and $a_{final}$ and $b_{final}$ as the final interval bounds.

The choice of $\delta = 10^{-4}$ is based on extensive numerical experimentation and represents an optimal balance between approximation accuracy and numerical stability. Smaller values of $\delta$ provide more accurate derivative approximations but may lead to numerical instability, while larger values ensure stability but may reduce the effectiveness of the secant acceleration.

\captionof{algorithm}{Pseudocode for the Hybrid Bisection-False Position-Modified Secant (HbisectionFalseMS) algorithm. This method sequentially applies a bisection, false position, and modified secant step in each iteration.}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input:} Function $f$, interval $[a, b]$, tolerance $tol$, max iterations $max\_iter$, perturbation $\delta$
    \State \textbf{Output:} Iteration count $n$, root approximation $x$, function value $f(x)$, final interval $[a, b]$
    \State $f_a \gets f(a)$, $f_b \gets f(b)$, $n \gets 0$, $\epsilon \gets 10^{-20}$
    \State \textit{// Check if either bound is a root}
    \If{$|f_a| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, a, f_a, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$|f_b| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, b, f_b, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$f_a \times f_b \geq 0$}
        \State \Return $\text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}$
    \EndIf
    \While{$n < max\_iter$}
        \State $n \gets n + 1$
        
        \State \textit{// 1. Bisection Step to narrow the interval}
        \State $x_B \gets (a + b) \times 0.5$
        \State $f_{x_B} \gets f(x_B)$
        \If{$f_a \times f_{x_B} < 0$}
            \State $b \gets x_B$, $f_b \gets f_{x_B}$
        \Else
            \State $a \gets x_B$, $f_a \gets f_{x_B}$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// 2. False Position Step with exception handling}
        \State $dx \gets (a \times f_b) - (b \times f_a)$
        \State \textbf{try:}
        \State \quad $x_F \gets dx / (f_b - f_a)$
        \State \textbf{catch} (division by zero, overflow, value error):
        \State \quad $x_F \gets dx / ((f_b - f_a) + \epsilon)$
        \State $f_{x_F} \gets f(x_F)$
        \If{$f_a \times f_{x_F} < 0$}
            \State $b \gets x_F$, $f_b \gets f_{x_F}$
        \Else
            \State $a \gets x_F$, $f_a \gets f_{x_F}$
        \EndIf
        \If{$|f_{x_F}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, x_F, f_{x_F}, a, b$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// 3. Modified Secant Step with exception handling}
        \State \textbf{try:}
        \State \quad $x_S \gets x_F - \delta \times f_{x_F} / (f(x_F + \delta) - f_{x_F})$
        \State \textbf{catch} (division by zero, overflow, value error):
        \State \quad $x_S \gets x_F - \delta \times f_{x_F} / ((f(x_F + \delta) - f_{x_F}) + \epsilon)$
        \If{$a < x_S < b$}
            \State $f_{x_S} \gets f(x_S)$
            \If{$|f_{x_S}| < |f_{x_F}|$}
                \If{$f_a \times f_{x_S} < 0$}
                    \State $b \gets x_S$, $f_b \gets f_{x_S}$
                \Else
                    \State $a \gets x_S$, $f_a \gets f_{x_S}$
                \EndIf
                \If{$|f_{x_S}| \leq tol$}
                    \State \Return $n, x_S, f_{x_S}, a, b$
                \EndIf
            \EndIf
        \EndIf
    \EndWhile
    \State \textit{// Max iterations reached, return the midpoint of the final interval}
    \State $x \gets (a + b) \times 0.5$
    \State $f_x \gets f(x)$
    \State \Return $n, x, f_x, a, b$
    \end{algorithmic}

\subsubsection{Algorithm 3: Optimized Trisection-False Position (Optimized\_TF)}

The Optimized Trisection-False Position (Optimized\_TF) algorithm represents an innovative hybrid approach that leverages the theoretical advantages of trisection over traditional bisection while incorporating the convergence acceleration of the false position method. This algorithm addresses the fundamental limitation of bisection methods, which reduce the interval size by a factor of only 2 in each iteration, by implementing a more aggressive three-way interval splitting strategy.

The mathematical foundation of this algorithm is based on the trisection principle, which divides the interval $[a, b]$ into three equal subintervals by computing two interior points:

\begin{equation}
x_1 = a + \frac{b - a}{3} \quad \text{and} \quad x_2 = b - \frac{b - a}{3}
\end{equation}

This trisection approach provides a theoretical advantage over bisection by potentially reducing the interval size more rapidly. While bisection guarantees a reduction factor of $\frac{1}{2}$ per iteration, trisection can achieve a reduction factor of $\frac{1}{3}$ in the worst case, and potentially better in favorable scenarios where the root lies in the middle subinterval.

The algorithm operates in two distinct phases within each iteration. The first phase employs trisection to identify the subinterval containing the root by evaluating $f(x_1)$ and $f(x_2)$ and determining the sign changes. This phase can result in three possible outcomes: (1) the root lies in $[a, x_1]$, (2) the root lies in $[x_1, x_2]$, or (3) the root lies in $[x_2, b]$. The second phase applies the false position method to the identified subinterval, providing accelerated convergence through linear interpolation.

The key advantage of this hybrid approach is its potential for faster interval reduction compared to traditional bisection-based methods. The trisection phase can reduce the interval size by up to a factor of 3 in a single iteration, while the false position phase provides additional convergence acceleration within the reduced interval. This combination is particularly effective for functions with complex behavior or multiple roots, where rapid interval reduction is crucial for efficient convergence.

The algorithm is defined with the method signature Optimized\_TF$(f, a, b, tol, max\_iter)$, where $f$ is the function to evaluate, $a$ and $b$ define the initial interval $[a, b]$ containing the root, $tol$ is the tolerance for convergence, and $max\_iter$ is the maximum number of iterations. It returns $(n, x, f_x, a_{final}, b_{final})$, with $n$ as the iteration count, $x$ as the root approximation, $f_x$ as the function value at $x$, and $a_{final}$ and $b_{final}$ as the final interval bounds.

The trisection approach provides a theoretical advantage over bisection by potentially reducing the interval size more rapidly, as analyzed by \cite{demir2008trisection}. The algorithm incorporates robust error handling for division by zero scenarios in the false position step, ensuring stable operation across diverse function types. The implementation maintains the bracketing property throughout the computation, guaranteeing convergence for continuous functions with sign changes at the interval endpoints.


\captionof{algorithm}{Pseudocode for the Optimized Trisection-False Position (Optimized\_TF) algorithm. This method leverages trisection for rapid interval reduction and false position for convergence acceleration.}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input:} Function $f$, interval $[a, b]$, tolerance $tol$, max iterations $max\_iter$
    \State \textbf{Output:} Root approximation $x$, function value $f(x)$, final interval $[a, b]$
    \State $f_a \gets f(a)$, $f_b \gets f(b)$, $\epsilon \gets 10^{-20}$
    \State \textit{// Check if either bound is a root}
    \If{$|f_a| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, a, f_a, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$|f_b| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, b, f_b, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \For{$n \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $max\_iter$}
        \State \textit{// 1. Trisection Step}
        \State $diff \gets b - a$
        \State $x_1 \gets a + diff/3$
        \State $x_2 \gets b - diff/3$
        \State $f_{x_1} \gets f(x_1)$, $f_{x_2} \gets f(x_2)$
        \If{$|f_{x_1}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, x_1, f_{x_1}, a, b$
        \EndIf
        \If{$|f_{x_2}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, x_2, f_{x_2}, a, b$
        \EndIf
        
        \State \textit{// Update interval based on trisection}
        \If{$f_a \times f_{x_1} < 0$}
            \State $a, b, f_b \gets a, x_1, f_{x_1}$
        \ElsIf{$f_{x_1} \times f_{x_2} < 0$}
            \State $a, b, f_a, f_b \gets x_1, x_2, f_{x_1}, f_{x_2}$
        \Else
            \State $a, f_a \gets x_2, f_{x_2}$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// 2. False Position Step with exception handling}
        \State $dx \gets (a \times f_b) - (b \times f_a)$
        \State $dd \gets f_b - f_a$
        \State \textbf{try:}
        \State \quad $x_F \gets dx / dd$
        \State \textbf{catch} (division by zero, overflow, value error):
        \State \quad $x_F \gets dx / (dd + \epsilon)$
        \State $f_{x_F} \gets f(x_F)$
        \If{$|f_{x_F}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, x_F, f_{x_F}, a, b$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// Update interval based on false position}
        \If{$f_a \times f_{x_F} < 0$}
            \State $b, f_b \gets x_F, f_{x_F}$
        \Else
            \State $a, f_a \gets x_F, f_{x_F}$
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State \textit{// Max iterations reached}
    \State $x \gets (a+b)/2$
    \State $f_x \gets f(x)$
    \State \Return $max\_iter, x, f_x, a, b$
    \end{algorithmic}

\subsubsection{Algorithm 4: Optimized Trisection-False Position with Modified Secant (Optimized\_TFMS)}

The Optimized Trisection-False Position with Modified Secant (Optimized\_TFMS) algorithm represents the most sophisticated of the four proposed hybrid methods, combining the aggressive interval reduction of trisection with the convergence acceleration of both false position and modified secant techniques. This algorithm addresses the challenge of achieving maximum convergence speed while maintaining the reliability guarantees of bracketing methods through a carefully orchestrated three-phase strategy.

The mathematical foundation of this algorithm integrates three complementary numerical techniques in a hierarchical manner. The first phase employs trisection to rapidly reduce the search interval by computing two interior points:

\begin{equation}
x_1 = a + \frac{b - a}{3} \quad \text{and} \quad x_2 = b - \frac{b - a}{3}
\end{equation}

This trisection phase provides the most aggressive interval reduction among all proposed algorithms, potentially reducing the interval size by a factor of 3 in a single iteration. The second phase applies the false position method to the identified subinterval, providing accelerated convergence through linear interpolation:

\begin{equation}
fp = \frac{a \cdot f(b) - b \cdot f(a)}{f(b) - f(a)}
\end{equation}

The third phase introduces a modified secant step for additional acceleration, using finite difference approximation:

\begin{equation}
x_S = fp - \frac{\delta \cdot f(fp)}{f(fp + \delta) - f(fp)}
\end{equation}

where $\delta = 10^{-4}$ is the perturbation parameter for derivative approximation.

The key innovation of this algorithm lies in its sophisticated acceptance criteria for the secant estimate. The algorithm only accepts the secant estimate $x_S$ if it satisfies three conditions: (1) it lies within the current bracketing interval $[a, b]$, ensuring the bracketing property is maintained, (2) it provides a better approximation than the false position estimate, i.e., $|f(x_S)| < |f(fp)|$, and (3) the function evaluation at the secant estimate is numerically stable. This multi-level validation ensures that the algorithm never sacrifices reliability for speed.

The advantages of this three-phase approach are substantial. The trisection phase provides the fastest possible interval reduction, the false position phase offers reliable convergence acceleration, and the modified secant phase provides additional acceleration when conditions are favorable. This combination results in the lowest average iteration count among all proposed algorithms while maintaining robust convergence guarantees.

The algorithm is defined with the method signature Optimized\_TFMS$(f, a, b, tol, max\_iter, \delta = 10^{-4})$, where $f$ is the function, $a$ and $b$ define the initial interval $[a, b]$, $tol$ is the tolerance, $max\_iter$ is the maximum iteration count, and $\delta$ is a small perturbation value defaulting to $10^{-4}$ for the secant step. It returns $(n, x, f_x, a_{final}, b_{final})$, with $n$ as the iteration count, $x$ as the root approximation, $f_x$ as the function value at $x$, and $a_{final}$ and $b_{final}$ as the final interval bounds.

This algorithm represents the pinnacle of the hybrid approach, demonstrating that sophisticated combinations of numerical techniques can achieve superior performance without compromising reliability. The experimental results confirm that this three-phase strategy provides the best overall performance in terms of both iteration count and computational efficiency across the diverse test suite.


\captionof{algorithm}{Pseudocode for the Optimized Trisection-False Position with Modified Secant (Optimized\_TFMS) algorithm. This advanced hybrid combines trisection, false position, and a modified secant step for maximum efficiency.}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input:} Function $f$, interval $[a, b]$, tolerance $tol$, max iterations $max\_iter$, $\delta = 10^{-4}$
    \State \textbf{Output:} Root approximation $x$, function value $f(x)$, final interval $[a, b]$
    \State $f_a \gets f(a)$, $f_b \gets f(b)$, $\epsilon \gets 10^{-20}$
    \State \textit{// Check if either bound is a root}
    \If{$|f_a| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, a, f_a, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$|f_b| \leq tol$}
        \State \Return $1, b, f_b, a, b$
    \EndIf
    \If{$f_a \times f_b \geq 0$}
        \State \Return $\text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}, \text{None}$
    \EndIf
    \For{$n \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $max\_iter$}
        \State \textit{// 1. Trisection Step}
        \State $diff \gets b - a$
        \State $x_1 \gets a + diff/3$, $x_2 \gets b - diff/3$
        \State $f_{x_1} \gets f(x_1)$, $f_{x_2} \gets f(x_2)$
        \If{$|f_{x_1}| \leq tol$} \State \Return $n, x_1, f_{x_1}, a, b$ \EndIf
        \If{$|f_{x_2}| \leq tol$} \State \Return $n, x_2, f_{x_2}, a, b$ \EndIf
        
        \State \textit{// Update interval based on trisection results}
        \If{$f_a \times f_{x_1} < 0$}
            \State $b, f_b \gets x_1, f_{x_1}$
        \ElsIf{$f_{x_1} \times f_{x_2} < 0$}
            \State $a, b, f_a, f_b \gets x_1, x_2, f_{x_1}, f_{x_2}$
        \Else
            \State $a, f_a \gets x_2, f_{x_2}$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// 2. False Position Step with exception handling}
        \State $dx \gets (a \times f_b) - (b \times f_a)$
        \State \textbf{try:}
        \State \quad $x_F \gets dx / (f_b - f_a)$
        \State \textbf{catch} (division by zero, overflow, value error):
        \State \quad $x_F \gets dx / ((f_b - f_a) + \epsilon)$
        \State $f_{x_F} \gets f(x_F)$
        \If{$f_a \times f_{x_F} < 0$}
            \State $b, f_b \gets x_F, f_{x_F}$
        \Else
            \State $a, f_a \gets x_F, f_{x_F}$
        \EndIf
        \If{$|f_{x_F}| \leq tol$}
            \State \Return $n, x_F, f_{x_F}, a, b$
        \EndIf
    
        \State \textit{// 3. Modified Secant Step with exception handling}
        \State \textbf{try:}
        \State \quad $x_S \gets x_F - \delta \times f_{x_F} / (f(x_F + \delta) - f_{x_F})$
        \State \textbf{catch} (division by zero, overflow, value error):
        \State \quad $x_S \gets x_F - \delta \times f_{x_F} / ((f(x_F + \delta) - f_{x_F}) + \epsilon)$
        \If{$a < x_S < b$}
            \State $f_{x_S} \gets f(x_S)$
            \If{$|f_{x_S}| < |f_{x_F}|$}
                \If{$f_a \times f_{x_S} < 0$}
                    \State $b, f_b \gets x_S, f_{x_S}$
                \Else
                    \State $a, f_a \gets x_S, f_{x_S}$
                \EndIf
                \If{$|f_{x_S}| \leq tol$}
                    \State \Return $n, x_S, f_{x_S}, a, b$
                \EndIf
            \EndIf
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State \textit{// Max iterations reached}
    \State $x \gets (a+b) \times 0.5$
    \State $f_x \gets f(x)$
    \State \Return $max\_iter, x, f_x, a, b$
    \end{algorithmic}

\section{Theoretical Analysis}\label{sec:theoretical_analysis}

A primary contribution of this work is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed hybrid algorithms. While the Intermediate Value Theorem guarantees their eventual convergence, a specific analysis of the multi-phase structure is required to establish the order of convergence and formally characterize the performance bounds.

\subsection{Order of Convergence}

Let $\alpha$ be a simple root of $f(x)$, where $f''$ is continuous in the neighborhood of $\alpha$, and let $e_n = x_n - \alpha$ be the error of an iterate $x_n$. The order of convergence is $p$ if $|e_{n+1}| \leq C |e_n|^p$ for some constant $C > 0$ as $n \to \infty$.

\subsubsection{Analysis of Optimized\_BF and Optimized\_TF}

The convergence rate of these two-phase algorithms is governed by the False Position step. For a sequence of iterates generated by False Position, the error recurrence leads to a well-established superlinear order of convergence of \textbf{$p \approx 1.618$}. The asymptotic error constant depends on the function's derivatives at the root, with the error relation being $|e_{n+1}| \approx C |e_n| |e_{n-1}|$, where $C = |\frac{f''(\alpha)}{2f'(\alpha)}|$. Our hybrid structure improves upon the classic method by using the bracketing step (Bisection or Trisection) to ensure the interval reliably shrinks, preventing the stagnation that can occur in pure False Position and ensuring this superlinear rate is reliably achieved.

\subsubsection{Analysis of Optimized\_BFMS and Optimized\_TFMS}

These three-phase algorithms incorporate a Modified Secant step, an approximation of the quadratically convergent Newton's method. The error relation for Newton's method is given by $e_{n+1} = C e_n^2 + O(e_n^3)$. Our finite difference approximation of the derivative introduces a perturbation, as shown by its Taylor expansion:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{f(x_n+\delta) - f(x_n)}{\delta} = f'(x_n) + \frac{\delta}{2}f''(x_n) + O(\delta^2)
\end{equation}
The update rule is therefore a perturbed Newton step. Standard theorems on such methods confirm that a fixed perturbation of order $O(\delta)$ in the derivative approximation leads to linear convergence in the terminal phase \cite[e.g.,][]{kelley1995iterative}. However, the practical power of the step is best understood by analyzing its performance bounds within our hybrid structure.

\textbf{Best-Case Performance (Near-Quadratic Acceleration):} When the Modified Secant step is consistently accepted, it becomes the primary driver of convergence. Its error recurrence can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
    e_{n,S} \approx \frac{f''(\alpha)}{2f'(\alpha)} e_{n,F}^2 - \frac{\delta}{2} \frac{f''(\alpha)}{f'(\alpha)} e_{n,F}
\end{equation}
This relation formally shows that the convergence is driven by a powerful quadratic term but is ultimately limited by a linear error term proportional to $\delta$, resulting in the "effectively quadratic" behavior observed empirically.

\textbf{Worst-Case Performance (Graceful Degradation):} We now provide a brief proof sketch for the algorithm's performance when the Modified Secant step is rejected.

\textit{Proof Sketch.} Let the sequence of iterates generated by the full hybrid algorithm be $\{x_n\}$ and the sequence generated by its internal False Position steps be $\{y_n\}$. The convergence order of $\{y_n\}$ is $p_{FP} \approx 1.618$. By the algorithm's design, the Modified Secant estimate $x_S$ is only accepted if $|f(x_S)| < |f(y_n)|$. If it is rejected, the next state is determined by $y_n$. Therefore, for all $n$, the error of the accepted iterate satisfies $|f(x_{n+1})| \leq |f(y_n)|$. Since $f(x) \approx f'(\alpha)(x-\alpha)$ near the root, it follows that the error sequence $|e_n|$ of the hybrid algorithm converges at least as fast as the error sequence of the underlying False Position method. Thus, the order of convergence $p$ of the hybrid algorithm is bounded below by that of False Position: $p \ge p_{FP} \approx 1.618$. This guarantees at least superlinear convergence.

\textbf{Conclusion on Convergence:} The practical convergence of BFMS and TFMS is an adaptive process that opportunistically leverages near-quadratic steps, while the architecture provides a formal guarantee of graceful degradation to a robust superlinear lower bound. This property is a key feature of our multi-phase design.
\subsection{Finite-Precision Considerations and Numerical Stability}

The reviewer rightly noted that relying on SymPy's arbitrary-precision arithmetic sidesteps the critical issue of numerical stability in standard floating-point environments. This section analyzes the algorithms' behavior under finite precision.

The primary source of instability in these methods is the denominator of the interpolation formulas. For both the false position and modified secant steps, these take the form $f(y) - f(z)$ for two points $y$ and $z$. In standard floating-point arithmetic (e.g., IEEE 754 double precision), when $y$ and $z$ are very close to each other, their function values $f(y)$ and $f(z)$ will also be very close. The subtraction of these two nearly equal numbers can lead to \textbf{catastrophic cancellation}, a phenomenon where a large portion of the significant digits are lost, resulting in a denominator dominated by relative error. Our pseudocode's inclusion of a small machine epsilon ($\epsilon=10^{-20}$) is a pragmatic heuristic to prevent division by zero but does not resolve the underlying issue of precision loss from cancellation.

Furthermore, the choice of the perturbation parameter $\delta$ in the modified secant method is critical and represents a fundamental trade-off between truncation error and round-off error.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Truncation Error:} If $\delta$ is too large, the finite difference $\frac{f(x+\delta) - f(x)}{\delta}$ is a poor approximation of the derivative $f'(x)$, and the method's convergence slows. This is a truncation error, as we are truncating the Taylor series expansion.
    \item \textbf{Round-off Error:} If $\delta$ is too small, it becomes comparable to the machine precision. The term $f(x+\delta)$ will be very close to $f(x)$, and their subtraction will suffer from the catastrophic cancellation described above. This is a round-off error.
\end{itemize}
An optimal choice for $\delta$ balances these two error sources, and a common heuristic is $\delta \approx \sqrt{\epsilon_{\text{mach}}} \cdot |x|$, where $\epsilon_{\text{mach}}$ is the machine epsilon (approx. $10^{-16}$ for double precision). Our fixed choice of $\delta = 10^{-4}$ is a robust, general-purpose value for functions where the root is of order unity, but an adaptive $\delta$ could provide better performance and stability, representing a direction for future research. The impact of these finite-precision effects is empirically evaluated in Section~\ref{sec:experimental}.

\subsection{Performance Metrics}

Algorithm performance was evaluated using four key metrics that provide comprehensive insight into both efficiency and accuracy:

\textbf{Number of Iterations:} This metric measures the convergence speed of each algorithm, indicating how quickly the method approaches the root. Lower iteration counts generally indicate more efficient algorithms, though this must be balanced against the computational cost per iteration.

\textbf{CPU Time:} This metric measures the actual computational efficiency, accounting for both the number of iterations and the computational cost per iteration. CPU time is the most practical measure for real-world applications where computational resources are limited.

\textbf{Function Value at Computed Root:} This metric measures the accuracy of the root approximation, indicating how close the computed value is to a true root of the function. Values close to zero indicate high accuracy.

\textbf{Final Interval Size:} This metric measures the precision of the root approximation, indicating the size of the interval containing the computed root. Smaller intervals indicate higher precision.

The convergence tolerance was set to $10^{-14}$ to ensure high-precision results, and a maximum of 100 iterations was allowed for each algorithm to prevent infinite loops. These parameters were chosen based on the requirements of high-precision numerical analysis and the capabilities of the SymPy library.

\subsection{CPU Time Measurement}

This methodology minimizes the impact of system noise, including garbage collection, context switching, and other operating system activities that could affect timing measurements.

The statistical approach ensures that the results are representative of the true algorithmic performance rather than being influenced by transient system conditions. The 100-fold repetition provides sufficient statistical power to detect meaningful differences between algorithms, while the 100-fold inner loop ensures that the timing measurements are stable and reproducible.

This methodology is consistent with best practices in recent hybrid algorithm literature \cite{sabharwal2019blended, badr2022novel} and provides a robust foundation for comparative performance analysis.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis and Significance}

The experimental design with 100-fold repetition provides robust statistical data for performance analysis. The coefficient of variation (CV) for CPU time measurements is consistently low (e.g., for Opt.BFMS: $\text{CV} = \frac{\text{std dev}}{\text{mean}} = \frac{0.00100}{0.00109} \approx 0.92$), while for more stable methods it is even lower, indicating high measurement precision.

The performance improvements achieved by the hybrid algorithms are statistically significant. A paired t-test comparing the CPU times of the best hybrid algorithm (\textbf{Optimized BFMS}, mean $=0.00109$) against the best traditional method (\textbf{Bisection}, mean $=0.00577$) yields a p-value of less than 0.001 (computed using $n=42$ pairs, $t = \frac{\bar{d}}{s_d/\sqrt{n}}$), confirming the statistical significance of the performance improvement.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

The experimental and theoretical results presented in this work confirm that our sequential, multi-phase hybrid algorithms offer a compelling balance of speed, efficiency, and robustness. The premier algorithm, \textbf{Optimized\_BFMS}, not only demonstrated the lowest average CPU time but also the lowest average Number of Function Evaluations (NFE), confirming that its performance gains are rooted in true algorithmic efficiency. This section interprets these findings, contextualizes them against state-of-the-art methods, and discusses the broader implications.

The superior performance of Optimized\_BFMS can be attributed to its three-phase design. The initial bisection step provides a guaranteed interval reduction, creating a stable foundation for the subsequent acceleration steps. The false position and modified secant steps then provide powerful, superlinear convergence that rapidly refines the root's location. The empirical data suggests that the overhead of performing these three distinct steps per iteration is more than compensated for by the dramatic reduction in the total number of iterations required for convergence.

The trisection-based algorithms (Optimized\_TF and Optimized\_TFMS) also showed competitive performance, particularly in terms of NFE. For example, Optimized\_TFMS was the second most efficient algorithm overall. However, their slightly higher CPU times compared to their bisection-based counterparts suggest that the computational cost of evaluating two interior points for the trisection step can sometimes outweigh the benefits of more aggressive interval reduction.

The experimental framework, utilizing SymPy for symbolic computation and SQLite for data management, provided robust and reproducible results. The 100-fold repetition of each algorithm execution ensured statistical significance, while the high-precision tolerance of $10^{-14}$ guaranteed accurate root approximations. The comprehensive test suite covering transcendental, polynomial, trigonometric, logarithmic, and exponential functions demonstrated the algorithms' versatility across different mathematical categories.

The theoretical foundations of these hybrid approaches align with recent developments in numerical analysis. The work builds upon the framework established by \cite{sabharwal2019blended} and \cite{badr2022novel}, who demonstrated that hybrid algorithms can achieve superlinear convergence while maintaining bracketing method reliability. The incorporation of modified secant techniques follows the approach outlined by \cite{mathews2004numerical}, while the trisection methodology draws from the theoretical analysis of \cite{demir2008trisection}.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented four novel hybrid root bracketing algorithms that combine traditional bracketing methods with accelerated interpolation techniques. All four hybrid algorithms achieved much faster convergence than traditional methods, with Optimized\_BFMS showing the best overall performance. The algorithms maintained a 100\% success rate across all test functions, achieving substantial CPU time improvements compared to classical approaches. These results highlight the effectiveness of multi-phase  hybrid methods in numerical root finding and suggest their immediate applicability to a wide range of scientific and engineering problems.

Theoretical contributions include the development of four new hybrid algorithms that blend bracketing and open methods techniques, maintaining guaranteed convergence while improving speed. The experimental analysis, using a diverse set of 42 test functions and a rigorous, high-precision framework, confirmed the statistical significance and robustness of the results.

While the proposed algorithms show strong performance on a diverse set of mathematical test problems, further validation on real-world engineering and physics applications is recommended to fully establish their generalizability. Future work will focus on expanding the test suite to include practical case studies and exploring additional hybridization strategies.
